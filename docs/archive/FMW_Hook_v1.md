## VERSION A — SITE PAGE
*Target: 200 words. Works as standalone Lens 0 page.*
*Tone: Direct, non-theoretical, accessible. No jargon.*

---

Maybe you're falling behind and can't explain why. Maybe you're doing
fine but sense the foundation beneath you is less stable than it looks.
Either way, something doesn't add up.

Wages rise. Prices rise faster. You save carefully and still fall behind.
You work harder and the gap doesn't close. Your parents bought a house
on one income. That math no longer works and nobody has a satisfying
explanation.

Most people blame someone. Politicians, corporations, the wealthy, the
other party. The blame feels justified. But it doesn't explain the
mechanism — and changing the people in charge has never fixed it.
Different faces, same result.

Here is what most people are never told: the problem is not the people.
It is the structure.

The monetary system — the invisible architecture beneath every price,
every wage, every savings account — is designed in a way that
redirects wealth — quietly, automatically, every year — from the
general population toward those at the top of the financial system.
Not through corruption, not through conspiracy. Through mechanism.
Because of how it is built. Any person, however well-intentioned,
placed inside that structure faces the same incentives and produces
the same outcomes. The structure is the problem.

The tools to replace it exist.
What's missing is broad understanding — and without it, we remain
trapped in a system of our own making.

*That's what this site is for. Start with* [The Lens →]

## VERSION B — ESSAY OPENING
*Target: 300 words. Replaces or precedes the current essay opening.*
*Slightly more developed — sets up the purchasing power argument.*

---

Maybe you're falling behind and can't explain why. Maybe you're doing
fine but sense the foundation beneath you is less stable than it looks.
Either way, something doesn't add up.

Wages rise. Prices rise faster. You work as hard as your parents did
and find yourself further behind. You save carefully, avoid debt,
make reasonable decisions — and the math still doesn't work the way
it was supposed to. Your kids look at housing prices and do the
calculation. It doesn't close.

Most people blame someone. Politicians, corporations, the wealthy,
the other side. The blame feels proportionate to the frustration.
But something is missing from that explanation: changing the people
in charge has never fixed it. Administrations change. The gap
between earning and affording keeps widening. Different faces,
same trajectory.

Here is what most people are never told: the problem is not the
people. It is the structure.

Beneath every price, every wage, every savings account is a monetary
architecture that redirects wealth — quietly, automatically, every
year — from the general population toward those at the top of the
financial system. Not through corruption, not through conspiracy.
Through mechanism. Any person, however well-intentioned, placed
inside that structure faces the same incentives and produces the
same outcomes. The constraint that would prevent it was removed
in 1971. Since then, the direction of travel has been consistent.

This is not a political argument. It is an architectural one.
The tools to replace this architecture exist. What's missing is
broad understanding — and without it, we remain trapped in a
system of our own making.

FreeMarketWatch was built to close that gap. To make the
architecture visible, to show what the economy looks like when
measured honestly, and to make the case for a structure that
is not only possible but already emerging.

That case begins with one question: what is purchasing power
actually doing?

## NOTES FOR ITERATION

**What the hook does:**
- Opens with universal recognition — the feeling, not the theory
- Names the instinct (blame someone) then redirects it (structure, not people)
- Delivers the structural insight without naming Bitcoin, THM, or any mechanism
- Ends with a forward pull — there's an explanation, here's where it starts

**What it deliberately omits:**
- Centralization (belongs in Lens 1 — the hook points toward it without naming it)
- THM, M2/GDP, any methodology
- Bitcoin (earned later, not assumed)
- Any policy prescription

**The centralization argument** enters naturally as the first major
point in Lens 1: why the structure produces this outcome, why
centralizing monetary authority creates the capability that gets
abused, and why distributing that authority — as Bitcoin does —
changes the incentive structure entirely. The hook creates the
appetite. Lens 1 satisfies it.

**Testing note:**
Run Version A with the local Bitcoin community first.
The questions they ask after reading it will tell you what
Lens 1 needs to lead with. Watch specifically for:
- Do they push back on "not the people"? (May need one more sentence)
- Do they ask "what is the structure?" (Good — that's the pull into Lens 1)
- Do they feel it's too vague? (May need one concrete example)
- Do comfortable readers feel implicated or alienated? (Key signal)

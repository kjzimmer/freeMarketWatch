## Why this site exists

Every financial chart you have ever seen measures value in dollars.
Returns in dollars. Prices in dollars. A currency that loses value
every year, with nobody marking the graduations.

When you measure everything in a shrinking ruler, you see a distorted
picture. Stocks that appear to gain value are often just keeping pace
with monetary debasement — or failing to. Currencies that appear
stable are quietly failing at their only job. Assets praised as safe
havens are losing real purchasing power against any honest benchmark.

This site exists because we think that distortion is the most
important and least discussed fact in finance.

**FreeMarketWatch shows financial markets through a different lens:
THM — Theoretical Hard Money.** Not what something costs in dollars,
but whether it genuinely preserves or gains purchasing power against
what honest money would look like. The fixed ruler, not the shrinking one.

When you see markets through this lens, the picture changes
dramatically. Most things that appear to be gaining value are
losing it. A handful of genuinely productive assets outperform.
And hard money — money that cannot be inflated away — sits
at the top of the chart.

The dashed green line on every chart is THM. Everything is
measured against it.

## What this site is building toward

Providing a different lens on existing markets is the first purpose
of this site. It is largely built.

The second purpose is larger and less finished: developing the
framework for how investors should think and act in a world where
hard money is the standard. What changes when your base currency
holds value? What information actually matters? What does a
genuinely THM-native investment analysis look like?

This is territory the standard financial literature has not mapped,
because that literature was built for a fiat world and takes fiat
assumptions for granted. We are developing it openly, in public,
as the site grows.

Both purposes live in **The Lens** — the intellectual foundation
of the site. Three components, honest about what is established
and what is still being worked through.

## How to read the charts

**The index (start = 100)**
Every chart starts everything at 100 — regardless of actual price.
This lets you compare a $2 commodity with a $500 stock on the same
scale. What matters is how much it changed, not what it costs.

**The THM line (dashed green) — the fixed ruler**
THM represents what money would look like if it had held its
purchasing power since 1913. We calculate it using M2 adjusted
for real economic output — the money supply growth above what
the growing economy actually needed. This is the most defensible
measure of pure monetary debasement we have found.

Everything above this line is genuinely gaining purchasing power.
Everything below it is losing ground — regardless of what the
dollar price says.

How we arrived at this calculation — and why it is genuinely
contested — is developed fully in Component 2 of The Lens.
[How we define THM →]

**The timeframes (1Y / 5Y / 10Y)**
Short timeframes are noisy. The 10-year view is where the real
story becomes visible — the slow, compounding effect of monetary
debasement on everything from currencies to savings to the cost
of daily life.

## What you are seeing in each panel

**World Currencies**
How major currencies have held — or lost — purchasing power
against THM. The dollar is the world reserve currency and the
primary instrument of debasement. Other currencies are largely
measured against the dollar — which means they are measured
against a shrinking ruler measuring against another shrinking
ruler. THM gives you the fixed reference point.

**Risk-Off Assets**
Instruments traditionally considered safe — gold, bonds, money
market instruments. Do they actually protect purchasing power
against THM over time? The answer is more complicated than
conventional wisdom suggests.

**Mag 7 / Risk-On**
The most productive public companies. Which genuinely outrun
monetary debasement? Which only appear to because we are
measuring in a shrinking currency? This is the central question
Component 3 of The Lens is developing.

## A note on the data and methodology

Data sources: Federal Reserve FRED, CoinGecko, Yahoo Finance.

THM is calculated using M2 adjusted for real GDP — excess money
supply growth above real economic output, base year 1913. We
believe this is the most theoretically grounded and empirically
validated measure of monetary debasement available. We do not
add a productivity adjustment — THM represents purchasing power
preservation, not purchasing power plus a productivity bonus.

We acknowledge this is not the only defensible answer. The
full methodology discussion — including the case for raw M2
as the philosophically purest measure, and why we did not
adopt it — is in Component 2 of The Lens.
